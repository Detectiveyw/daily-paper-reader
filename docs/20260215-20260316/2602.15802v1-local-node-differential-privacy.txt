# Local Node Differential Privacy 

## Sofya Raskhodnikova ‚ąó Adam Smith ‚ąó Connor Wagaman ‚ąó Anatoly Zavyalov ‚ąó

Abstract 

We initiate an investigation of node differential privacy for graphs in the local model of private data analysis. In our model, dubbed LNDP ‚čÜ, each node sees its own edge list and releases the output of a local randomizer on this input. These outputs are aggregated by an untrusted server to obtain a final output. We develop a novel algorithmic framework for this setting that allows us to accurately answer arbitrary linear queries on a blurry approximation of the input graph‚Äôs degree distribution. For some natural problems, the resulting algorithms match the accuracy achievable with node privacy in the central model, where data are held and processed by a trusted server. We also prove lower bounds on the error required by LNDP ‚čÜ that imply the optimality of our algorithms for several fundamental graph statistics. We then lift these lower bounds to the interactive LNDP setting, demonstrating the optimality of our algorithms even when constantly many rounds of interaction are permitted. Obtaining our lower bounds requires new approaches, since those developed for the usual local model do not apply to the inherently overlapping inputs that arise from graphs. Finally, we prove structural results that reveal qualitative differences between local node privacy and the standard local model for tabular data.

# 1 Introduction 

Differential privacy (DP) [DMNS16] is the standard framework for releasing statistics while protecting individuals‚Äô data. Industry deployments of DP often use the local model, where each client randomizes its data before it is collected [EPK14; BEM+17; DKY17; App23]. This model has been developed for tabular data [KLNRS11] and, more recently, considered for graph data. In the central model, differential privacy for graphs has been extensively studied with two canonical variants: edge privacy [NRS07], which, intuitively, guards against changing a single edge and is relevant when individuals correspond to edges, and node privacy 

[BBDS13; CZ13; KNRS13], which, intuitively, guards against changing the edges incident to one node and is relevant when individuals correspond to nodes. Many modern graph datasets containing sensitive information, such as social and communication networks, collaboration graphs, and contact-tracing graphs, are naturally distributed: each node knows its neighbors but no single authority sees the whole graph. Furthermore, in many such settings, nodes correspond to individuals. The sensitive unit is a person‚Äôs entire neighborhood, and even aggregate information about that neighborhood can be sensitive [JM09]. For example, in a graph of sexual relationships, an individual‚Äôs neighborhood is their set of sexual partners; protecting only individual edges would still allow for inference of intimate relationships and behavioral patterns. Thus, the natural notion of privacy protection in such settings is node-level, not just edge-level. We provide the first investigation of node DP in the local model, which captures the concerns and constraints of distributed social network data. Prior work on graphs in the local model has focused on edge DP [QYYKXR17; IMC21; IMC22a; DLRSSY22; ELRS25; MPSL25]. We formulate the new model, design algorithms under this strong privacy guarantee, develop new lower-bound techniques showing that our algorithms are near-optimal for several fundamental graph statistics, and prove structural results that reveal qualitative differences between local node privacy and the standard local model for tabular data. 

Local node differential privacy ( LNDP ‚čÜ) model. We study the local analogue of node differential privacy for graphs. There are n parties, each corresponding to a node and receiving its incident edge list as input. Each party runs a local randomizer on its own input, using both public and local randomness, and releases the output to an (untrusted) central server, which post-processes all reports to estimate the desired statistic. For privacy parameters őĶ and őī, the overall algorithm is ( őĶ, őī )-LNDP ‚čÜ (Definition 2.3) if the joint distribution over the outputs of all parties is ( őĶ, őī )-indistinguishable on every pair of node-neighboring graphs, i.e., graphs that differ in the edges incident to a single node. We study the error achievable by LNDP ‚čÜ algorithms for fundamental graph statistics. Some of our error guarantees are worst-case over all graphs, while others are conditional on a promise or distributional assumption on the input graph. However, in all cases, as is standard in the literature, we nevertheless require privacy for all input graphs; assumptions on the input are only needed for accuracy. The LNDP ‚čÜ model is noninteractive . We focus on this setting since it captures existing deployments of local DP and has been studied extensively for tabular data (e.g., in [DJW13; BS15; BNS19; ENU20; CGKM21; CGS26]). We also present an interactive formulation of the model (Definition 5.10), in the style presented by [KLNRS11; JMNR19] for tabular data, and show that our main lower bounds extend even to interactive LNDP algorithms with a constant number of rounds.

## 1.2 Our Techniques 

Our algorithms, lower bounds, and structural results require the development of new techniques tailored to LNDP ‚čÜ. Unlike in the local model for tabular data, the inputs of the parties in an LNDP ‚čÜ computation necessarily overlap: each edge appears in the views of both endpoints. Our algorithms account for, and even exploit, this overlap. However, our lower bounds show that, despite the correlations among the outputs of local randomizers induced by this overlap, LNDP ‚čÜ algorithms cannot reliably distinguish some very different graph families, implying that our algorithms for several tasks are essentially optimal.

Advanced grouposition for pure LNDP ‚čÜ Algorithms (Section 6). Theorem 6.1, on ‚Äúadvanced grouposition‚ÄĚ, demonstrates a separation between pure LNDP ‚čÜ and approximate LNDP ‚čÜ. ## 1.3 Related Work 

We draw most heavily from work on local privacy and central-model node privacy. We briefly review key results in these areas as well as seemingly related lines of work that differ from ours in significant ways. 7Differential privacy for graphs‚ÄĒin the setting of edge privacy‚ÄĒwas introduced by [NRS07]. The first nontrivial node-private algorithms appeared concurrently in [BBDS13; CZ13; KNRS13]: they achieved accuracy under a structural promise (e.g., bounded maximum degree) and then used Lipschitz extensions and projections to extend privacy to all graphs while retaining accuracy on instances satisfying the promise. This paradigm underlies most subsequent work on node privacy, which is powered by Lipschitz extensions [DLL16; RS16; BCSZ18; CD20; KRST23] and projections [DLL16; JSW24]. This work covers edge and subgraph counts [BBDS13; CZ13; KNRS13], degree distribution estimation [DLL16; RS16], connected component counts [KRST23; JSW24], and releasing generalizations of matching [DLLZ25]; and, for distributional tasks, includes parameter estimation for ErdňĚ os‚ÄďR¬ī enyi graphs [SU21; CDHS24], block models [CDdHLS24], and graphons [BCS15; BCSZ18]. Node-private algorithms have also been developed for the streaming (‚Äúcontinual release‚ÄĚ) setting [JSW24]. Our techniques necessarily depart from this approach: the indistinguishability of empty and regular graphs (Lemma 5.4) shows that the usual design paradigm for node-private algorithms fails in the local model. Local (LDP) algorithms can be traced back to Warner [War65] and were formalized by [DMNS16; KLNRS11; BNO08]. Connections to information theory (e.g., entropy, mutual information, and KL divergence) were developed by [DJW13] and extended in [BS15; ENU20; CGKM21]. Further investigation revealed the power of interactivity [JMNR19] and properties such as advanced grouposition and the equivalence of pure and approximate LDP [BNS19]. LDP has also seen large-scale deployments for tabular data [EPK14; BEM+17; DKY17; App23]. While we leverage some LDP tools for tabular data, correlations inherent to distributed graph data required developing new algorithmic techniques and new connections to information theory. Prior work on local privacy for graphs has focused entirely on edge privacy. Papers evoking ‚Äúlocal node privacy‚ÄĚ do not match our definition and do not align with the notion of node privacy in the central model: [QYYKXR17] protects only a node‚Äôs own edge list‚ÄĒand not the copies of the same edges held by its neighbors (in fact providing a definition equivalent to tabular LDP)‚ÄĒand develops only edge private algorithms, while [ZLBR20] protects node attributes under a public topology (with publicly known and thus unprotected edges). Work on local edge privacy began with an investigation of synthetic graphs [QYYKXR17] and subgraph counting [IMC21]. Later, [DLRSSY22] formalized the model and linked it to parallelizable graph algorithms. The first lower bounds specific to local edge-DP (that do not immediately follow from lower bounds for LDP) were shown by [ELRS25] for triangle counting. Finally, [MPSL25] advances practical local algorithms that satisfy edge privacy.

Our results raise several concrete future directions for LNDP ‚čÜ. The first is to completely pin down the error needed for edge counting: is the D‚ąön term in our upper bound of O (D‚ąön + n) ‚ąölog(1 /őī )

On the structural side, we proved that pure and approximate LNDP ‚čÜ are genuinely different, but we do not yet have a complete understanding of the gap between these models. Similarly, we have a separation between LNDP ‚čÜ algorithms that see full edge lists and those that see only degrees; strengthening this separation or finding additional tasks that witness it would clarify the relative power of different local views. ## 5.3 Impossibility Results for Interactive LNDP 

Our impossibility results for edge counting and ErdňĚ os‚ÄďR¬ī enyi parameter estimation also extend to interactive 

LNDP algorithms. An interactive LNDP algorithm proceeds by rounds, in which each node runs a per-node algorithm that takes as input its local neighborhood, public randomness, and outputs from itself and other nodes in previous rounds. Node privacy requires the overall transcript of randomizer outputs, choices of nodes, and choices of randomizers to be ( őĶ, őī )-indistinguishable on node-neighboring input graphs. The definition below follows the style of those in [KLNRS11; JMNR19; ELRS25] for the tabular and edge-privacy settings. 

Definition 5.10 (Interactive LNDP) . Let n, ‚Ąď ‚ąą N. A transcript ŌÄ is a vector consisting of some initial public randomness ŌĀ, and a 3-tuple for each round t ‚ąą [‚Ąď] of the form (StU , S tR, S tY ). Each element in the tuple encodes, respectively, the set StU ‚äÜ [n] of nodes chosen, the per-node algorithms 10 used by each of the chosen nodes (this description includes the per-node algorithm‚Äôs parameters‚ÄĒe.g., its privacy parameters), and the (randomized) output produced. An algorithm in this model is a function A that maps each possible transcript to public randomness, a set of nodes, and per-node algorithms for those nodes. Given őĶ > 0 and őī ‚ąą [0 , 1] , a randomized algorithm A satisfies (őĶ, őī )-local node differential privacy (LNDP) 

if the algorithm TA that outputs the entire transcript generated by A has the property that, for all choices of initial public randomness ŌĀ and all pairs of node-neighboring graphs G and G‚Ä≤ on node set [n],

TA(G) ‚ČąőĶ,őī TA(G‚Ä≤).

# 6 Advanced Grouposition for Pure LNDP ‚čÜ

In this section, we prove an analogue of ‚Äúadvanced grouposition‚ÄĚ for pure LNDP ‚čÜ. In the standard LDP setting for tabular data, advanced grouposition [BNS19] states that group privacy guarantees for k users degrade proportional to ‚ąök rather than k. We show an analogous result for pure LNDP ‚čÜ in Theorem 6.1, which we prove in Section 6.2. We also prove that advanced grouposition cannot apply to approximate LNDP ‚čÜ, showing a separation between the pure- and approximate- LNDP ‚čÜ settings‚ÄĒin contrast, [BNS19] show that pure and approximate LDP are essentially equivalent. Our proof of advanced grouposition requires insights specific to pure-LNDP ‚čÜ algorithms. In the standard local model, changing the data of k individuals only affects the inputs to those k randomizers, so only these randomizers‚Äô outputs contribute to the privacy loss. k nodes may affect all nodes‚Äô edge lists, and thus the inputs to all randomizers. However, we show that the brittle structure of pure LNDP ‚čÜ means that, even though many nodes‚Äô edge lists can change, only a few of these nodes‚Äô randomizers contribute significantly to the privacy loss.
