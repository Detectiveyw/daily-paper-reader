Abstract 

The increasing reliance on cloud-hosted Large Language Models (LLMs) exposes sensitive client data, such as prompts and responses, to potential privacy breaches by service providers. Existing approaches fail to ensure privacy, maintain model performance, and preserve computational efficiency simultaneously. To address this challenge, we propose Talaria, a confidential inference framework that parti-tions the LLM pipeline to protect client data without compromising the cloud’s model in-tellectual property or inference quality. Ta-laria executes sensitive, weight-independent operations within a client-controlled Confiden-tial Virtual Machine (CVM) while offloading weight-dependent computations to the cloud GPUs. The interaction between these environ-ments is secured by our Reversible Masked Outsourcing (ReMO) protocol, which uses a hybrid masking technique to reversibly obscure intermediate data before outsourcing computa-tions. Extensive evaluations show that Talaria can defend against state-of-the-art token infer-ence attacks, reducing token reconstruction ac-curacy from over 97.5% to an average of 1.34%, all while being a lossless mechanism that guar-antees output identical to the original model without significantly decreasing efficiency and scalability. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that ensures clients’ prompts and responses remain inaccessible to the cloud, while also preserving model privacy, perfor-mance, and efficiency. 

1 Introduction 

The widespread deployment of cloud-hosted Large Language Models (LLMs), such as Gemini 2.5 (Co-manici et al., 2025) and GPT 5 (OpenAI, 2025a), brings powerful capabilities but also an immediate privacy hazard. Client data, including prompts and model responses, often contains personal, finan-cial, or health information and, when processed on cloud, are exposed to the service provider (Fig. 1) (Gim et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2025a). This exposure has already chilled adoption across reg-ulated sectors (e.g., JPMorgan, Citi, and Gold-man Sachs restrictions (Farrell, 2023; Bushard, 2023); healthcare cautions about HIPAA compli-ance (Bruce, 2023)), underscoring that data confi-dentiality becomes a deployment prerequisite for trust. Beyond market signals, regulators likewise now expect systematic risk assessments: GDPR generally requires a Data Protection Impact Assess-ment (PIA) for high-risk processing, and authori-ties have enforced these obligations against LLM providers (Wolford, 2025). For example, ChatGPT was temporarily banned in Italy and later fined C15 million for training on personal data without an ad-equate legal basis (Pollina and Armellini, 2024). In parallel, U.S. FTC oversight is pushing PIA-like reviews for AI services (Bissex, 2025), and Que-bec’s Law 25 makes PIAs mandatory with signifi-cant penalties (LégisQuébec, 2021). Consequently, any trustworthy cloud-based LLM solution must provide end-to-end safeguards for prompts (and responses) to demonstrate PIA compliance. Prompt

Challenges. While ensuring prompt-response con-fidentiality, the primary challenge is satisfying three conflicting requirements, often termed the “impossible trinity” (Gim et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024): ① Model Privacy , which requires safeguard-ing client data without compromising the confiden-tiality of cloud-hosted models; ② Model Perfor-mance , which requires output invariance, i.e., that 1

new mechanisms do not alter the model’s outputs; 

③ Model Efficiency , which requires that privacy mechanisms introduce only modest computational overhead. This paper aims to realize a confidential inference mechanism that prevents the cloud from obtaining the client’s prompt and response while balancing the impossible trinity of model privacy, performance, and efficiency. 

Status Quo. Current approaches to private LLM inference uniformly fail to balance this trinity (Tbl. 1). Data-centric methods, including differen-tial privacy (Wu et al., 2024, 2025) and anonymiza-tion (Chen et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2024), com-promise performance by altering inputs, which vio-lates output invariance. Homomorphic encryption-based mechanisms (Liu and Liu, 2023; Huang et al., 2022) provide provable security, but the large com-putational overhead and the need to access model parameters limit their widespread application in LLMs. Confidential Virtual Machines (CVMs) with GPU support have been proposed as a path forward, promising greater efficiency by leverag-ing secure hardware enclaves. While CVMs can effectively isolate the runtime environment, exist-ing implementations remain vulnerable; they do not prevent leakage of model parameters and the response, thus failing to provide comprehensive privacy guarantees (Gim et al., 2024). 2 Related Work 

DP-based methods. Differential privacy (Dwork, 2006) offers an efficient way to protect client prompts by perturbing the inputs, but this often comes at the cost of performance. DP-based tech-niques inject noise into token distributions, em-beddings, or use paraphrasing to obscure original inputs (Wu et al., 2025, 2024; Hong et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2025b). While highly efficient and not requiring changes to the backend model, this noise injection inevitably creates a trade-off between pri-vacy and performance, as it can degrade the quality of downstream tasks. 

HE-based methods. Homomorphic Encryption, often used with secure multi-party computation, allows for direct computation on encrypted data, promising strong, provable security for LLM infer-ence without degrading output quality (Liu and Liu, 22023; Huang et al., 2022). However, the practical application of these methods to large-scale cloud-based LLMs is hindered by two major obstacles: prohibitive computational overhead that violates efficiency, and the need for model access, which compromises the model confidentiality. 

CVM-based methods. CVMs provide a hardware-based TEE to isolate client data, but practical im-plementation for LLMs remains challenging. The naive approach of running one full LLM instance per client CVM, while secure (Hanzlik et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2019), is unscalable and economically impractical due to the immense memory require-ments of LLMs. Hybrid solutions attempt to solve this by processing prompts inside the CVM while offloading token generation to the cloud (Gim et al., 2024). This improves efficiency but critically un-dermines the security model: it violates model con-fidentiality by exposing the model to the client and compromises client privacy by revealing the plain-text response to the cloud provider. 

3 Threat Model 

We consider a typical two-party cloud inference setting with: a cloud provider holds LLMs, and a client holds privacy-sensitive prompts and receives model responses. 

Cloud. Consistent with related work (Gim et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2025), we assume the cloud provider operates under the honest-but-curious 

threat model. The cloud is honest as it is econom-ically motivated to follow the inference protocol faithfully; its business model relies on customer trust, which would be destroyed by any detected malicious tampering (Sharma and Chen, 2021; Xue et al., 2018). Simultaneously, the cloud is curious ,meaning it may leverage its extensive computa-tional resources to analyze prompts and responses, aiming to reconstruct sensitive client information or user attributes via inversion attacks (Luo et al., 2025; Qu et al., 2025). 

Client. The client is also honest-but-curious : it adheres to the protocol to obtain inference results while attempting to learn the cloud’s proprietary pa-rameters (e.g., reverse-engineering model weights) from any messages it legitimately receives. 

Trusted Computing Base (CVM). Our secu-rity model is based on the availability of GPU-accelerated CVMs. This assumption is practical, as major cloud platforms like Amazon Web Ser-vices (AWS, 2025), Microsoft Azure (Azure, 2025), and Google Cloud (Google, 2025) already provide such offerings. The client can verify the integrity of a CVM through remote attestation. Once ver-ified, the CVM is considered a trusted extension of the client, allowing them to form a single logi-cal party and securely share secrets. The CVM’s runtime state is cryptographically isolated from the cloud; the provider cannot directly access plaintext data inside the CVM. While the cloud cannot read the CVM’s runtime data, it retains control over how the CVM is provisioned. Following indus-try practice (Russinovich, 2024; Martinez, 2024), the provider may enforce a minimal, pre-approved software stack and capability set to reduce misuse and to narrow the attack surface (e.g., blocking privileged or abuse-prone instructions and inter-faces (Schwarz and Rossow, 2024), or enforcing launch control policies that only allow signed en-clave binaries (Schwarz et al., 2020)). These con-trols prevent a curious client from turning the CVM into an oracle for model extraction or systems in-trospection, without granting the cloud visibility into the CVM’s plaintext. 

Out of scope. Following standard CVM prac-tice (Gim et al., 2024; Hanzlik et al., 2021), we ex-clude microarchitectural and side-channel attacks, physical attacks, supply-chain compromises, and denial-of-service. We also do not consider a fully malicious cloud that arbitrarily deviates from the protocol (see Sec. 7 for discussion).

4.2.1 One-time setup. 

The client remotely attests a CVM and establishes a secure channel. The provider loads the model on a public GPU. We then precompute a reusable restoration pool for later recovery (Sec. 4.4). 

Model partitioning. Each layer is split so that linear ops (e.g., Q/K/V and output projections in attention; MLP projections) execute on the unpro-tected cloud GPU, while structural decoding ops execute inside the CVM. 

Restoration pool preparation. The CVM gen-erates a public base Mpub ∈ Rm×d with m < d ,sends it to the GPU, and receives Rpub = Mpub W

back. Rpub will let the CVM later undo outsourced multiplications without ever obtaining W .

4.2.2 Iterative inference. 

For each decoding step, the client’s prompt X is decrypted inside the CVM and embedded to E.Before outsourcing any weight multiplication, the CVM masks E (Sec. 4.3), sends only the masked tensor to the unprotected GPU for weighted decod-ing, and then locally restores the exact plaintext result and proceeds with structural decoding. The private KV cache remains in the CVM. The process repeats until EOS ; see pseudocode in Appx. B.1. 

4.3 Reversible Masked Outsourcing (ReMO) 

Naively outsourcing EW reveals E to the cloud. ReMO prevents this with a reversible additive mask. Given E ∈ Rn×d and W ∈ Rd×d, the CVM sam-ples a secret mask M ∈ Rn×d and forms 

ˆE = E + M. 

Only ˆE is sent to the GPU, which computes 

ˆO = ˆEW = EW + M W. 

To recover the true result O = EW inside the CVM, we must subtract the restoration term R =

M W . The core challenge is that R depends jointly on the CVM secret M and the cloud secret W . Our protocol ensures neither secret ever leaves its trust boundary while still enabling exact restoration. 

Correctness (output invariance). ReMO is al-gebraically lossless: after restoration (Sec. 4.4), the CVM obtains exactly O = EW . Hence, under a fixed sampler, responses with and without Talaria are bitwise identical (validated in Sec. 6.4).

Hybrid masking separates mask generation from mask application so that the CVM can compute 

R = M W without W and the cloud can process masked data without M .

4.5 Security Challenge and Threat Focus 

Outsourcing weighted decoding naively exposes E

to a cloud adversary A, enabling prompt/response inversion X′ ← A (E) with X′ ≈ X. ReMO with hybrid masking removes this leakage by ensuring the GPU only ever sees masked tensors and never receives enough information to infer M (or undo it), while the CVM never obtains W .

5 Privacy Guarantee Analysis 

5.1 Data Confidentiality Adversary and domain. We protect client prompts and responses against an “honest-but-curious” cloud that may adaptively query the pro-tocol and analyze all public transcripts. We treat intermediate embeddings E ∈ Rn×d under a fixed, deterministic quantization into a finite ring (e.g., 

Z2k ) so that masking and indistinguishability are defined over a finite domain. All results extend to reals via standard discretization arguments. 

Mask generation and freshness. For each de-coding step (per token), the CVM samples a fresh, ephemeral private matrix Mpvt ∈ Rn×m and forms an additive mask M = Mpvt Mpub using a public base Mpub ∈ Rm×d established during setup. The mask is applied entrywise: ˆE = E +M . Freshness (single-use) and full-space randomness ensure the masked distribution does not concentrate on any known low-dimensional subspace.

5.2 Model Confidentiality Goal. Prevent a curious client from recovering proprietary weights W ∈ Rd×d.

Mathematical isolation. The client observes only the linearly transformed Rpub = Mpub W

with Mpub ∈ Rm×d and m < d. Since 

rank( Mpub ) ≤ m < d , the right null space is nontrivial, dim(ker( Mpub )) ≥ d − m > 0, and 

{W ′ : Mpub W ′ = Rpub } = (4) 

W0 + {Z : each column of Z ∈ ker( Mpub )}.

Thus the feasible set is an affine subspace of posi-tive dimension (infinitely many candidates), mak-ing unique recovery of W information-theoretically impossible. Here ker( M ) = {v ∈ Rd : M v = 0 }.

System-level hardening. We enforce remote attes-tation of the CVM image and restrict its capabilities to the minimal Talaria logic: no debug hooks, no general linear-algebra toolkits exposing arbitrary solves, and no interfaces that could be repurposed to probe Rpub beyond its intended use. Attestation binds code and configuration to a hash verified by the cloud, preventing a curious client from modi-fying the CVM to mount offline model-recovery oracles. See Appx. 6 Evaluations 

6.1 Evaluation Setup 

All evaluations are conducted on a cloud node with 8 Nvidia H800 with 80GB of memory, 10 AMD EPYC processor cores, and 6400 GB of sys-tem memory. We enable security features such as AMD SEV-SNP (AMD, 2023) and NVIDIA GPU CC (Nvidia, 2023) for evaluations that require confidential computing. Detailed evaluation setup (datasets, metrics, etc.) can be found in Appx. C.1. 

Datasets and models. We evaluate Talaria on four diverse datasets: Midjourney prompts (AI, 2024), WikiText-2 (Merity et al., 2017), Patient-notes (Ha et al., 2022), and GPT-OSS20B-samples (Morris, 2025). To simulate our threat model, we use 20% of each dataset as private client prompts and allow the remaining 80% to be used for training attack models. Our experiments leverage models from the Llama3 (1B, 8B, 70B) (Meta, 2025) and Qwen3 (0.6B, 8B, 32B) (Alibaba, 2025) families. By de-fault, we use Qwen3-8B for all evaluations unless stated otherwise. 

Metrics. For protection evaluation, we use To-ken Reconstruction Accuracy (TRA) and Cosine Semantic Similarity (CSS). The higher the TRA and CSS, the higher the similarity between the two texts, which can reflect the higher accuracy of the token reconstruction attack. For efficiency evalua-tion, we use end-to-end latency and Time to First Token (TTFT). 

Attacks. We validate our defense against three SOTA reconstruction attacks targeting in-termediate data: TokenInfer (Luo et al., 2025) (classification-based), TokenInv (Qu et al., 2025) (optimization-based), and SentenceInv (Gu et al., 2023) (generation-based). By default, we use To-kenInfer for most evaluations. According to Token-Infer, the reconstruction performance decreases as the number of layers increases. Therefore, in order to maximize the attack effect, we use the outputs of the first attention layer as the attack object. 

Baselines. We first use No Protection and Full Iso-lation as the baseline, where the former is normal unprotected cloud inference and the latter is where each client loads a copy of the complete LLM into CVM for inference. Then we also use two SOTA defense mechanisms as baselines: SPD (Gim et al., 2024), Cape (Wu et al., 2025).

7 Conclusion 

We introduced Talaria, a confidential inference framework for cloud-based LLMs by partition-ing the inference pipeline and leveraging CVMs and the ReMO protocol. Empirically, Talaria cuts reconstruction success to near-negligible levels across models, datasets, and attacks, while pre-serving bit-for-bit outputs and competitive latency. To the best of our knowledge, the first to keep both prompts and responses confidentiality while bal-ancing model privacy, performance, and efficiency. 8Limitations 

The limitations of this work mainly include the following two aspects: 

Threat Scenario. We adopt the standard honest-but-curious setting: the cloud provider executes the protocol correctly but may analyze observed artifacts to infer private information. This as-sumption is reasonable for cloud LLM services because ① providers are economically and reputa-tionally incentivized to follow deployed protocols, making arbitrary deviation unlikely in practice; ②

confidential-computing primitives (e.g., CVM at-testation and capability scoping) already constrain what either party can do while still enabling rou-tine operations; and ③ it aligns with the dominant threat model used in prior confidential-inference work (Gim et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2025) and in our system design. Under this setting, our guaran-tees are testable, and the resulting system remains practical to deploy at scale. We do not defend against a fully malicious or compromised cloud provider that can arbitrarily de-viate from the protocol (e.g., returning malformed messages or bypassing attested images). Defend-ing against such adversaries would require stronger mechanisms (e.g., verifiable execution, runtime au-diting, or side-channel–resilient defenses), which we leave to future work. 

Partial model protection. Talaria does not attempt to hide the entire model implementation from the client. Instead, it prioritizes weight confidential-ity : proprietary weight parameters remain on the cloud side, while weight-free operators (e.g., nor-malization, attention scoring/softmax, residual con-nections, and sampling) are executed within the CVM. This choice is deliberate and reasonable. In contemporary LLM stacks, the structure of these operators is largely standardized and often public, whereas the learned weight matrices encode the provider’s core intellectual property and present the primary model-extraction risk. By keeping all weight-dependent computations on the cloud provider’s hardware and exposing only weight-free control flow, the client may observe the computa-tion pattern but not the parameters. Moreover, visibility into non-weight operators does not enable parameter recovery under our pro-tocol. The client observes at most a fixed, under-determined sketch Rpub = Mpub W with m < d ,leaving a non-trivial right kernel and thus infinitely many W consistent with the observation; without additional side information, the true weights are non-identifiable. In contrast, fully obscuring every non-weight detail would require heavy obfusca-tion or cryptography, undermining our efficiency and output-invariance goals. Prioritizing weight confidentiality therefore strikes a practical balance among privacy, performance, and scalability, yield-ing bit-for-bit identical outputs while protecting the provider’s proprietary parameters.

B.4 Theoretical Analysis of Model Confidentiality 

This section provides a formal analysis of the mathematical guarantees that underpin Talaria’s model confidentiality, as introduced in the main paper. The core principle is to prevent a curi-ous client, who controls a CVM instance, from reverse-engineering the cloud’s proprietary model weights, W . This protection is achieved by struc-turing the interaction between the CVM and the public GPU as an underdetermined system of lin-ear equations, making the unique recovery of W

information-theoretically impossible. 

Problem formulation. During the one-time setup phase, the CVM receives a transformed version of the weight matrix: 

Rpub = Mpub W

where Mpub ∈ Rm×d is a publicly generated base mask, Rpub ∈ Rm×d is the published sketch, and 

W ∈ Rd×d are the secret model weights. From the client’s perspective, Mpub and Rpub are known, while W is unknown. 

Analysis as a system of linear equations. The matrix equation above can be decomposed into d

independent systems of linear equations, one for each column of W . Let wj and rj be the j-th columns of W and Rpub , respectively. For each column j ∈ { 1, . . . , d }, we have the following system: 

Mpub wj = rj

In this system, the matrix Mpub is the coefficient matrix, wj is the vector of d unknown variables (the weights in that column), and rj is the known result vector. 

Underdetermined constraint. The protocol en-forces m < d , so each system Mpub wj = rj is underdetermined. Since rank( Mpub ) ≤ m < d ,the null space is non-trivial, with 

dim(ker( Mpub )) ≥ d − m > 0

The notation ker( Mpub ) denotes the kernel (or null space) of a matrix Mpub , defined as 

ker( Mpub ) = {x ∈ Rd : Mpub x = 0 }

It represents the set of directions that are invisible to Mpub . If ker( Mpub ) is non-trivial, then any so-lution to Mpub x = b can be shifted by an element of ker( Mpub ) to produce another valid solution, yielding infinitely many possibilities. 

Solution space. Each solution set has the affine form: 

wj = wp + wh

where wp is a particular solution (e.g., wp =

M +pub rj with the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse), and wh ∈ ker( Mpub ). Thus, infinitely many can-didate weight vectors are consistent with the ob-served data. 

Proposition (Non-identifiability under no pri-ors). Let Sj = {wp + z : z ∈ ker( Mpub )} be the solution set for column wj . If no auxiliary con-straints on wj are available (i.e., the admissible set is Rd), then |S j | = ∞ and the true weight vec-tor is not uniquely identifiable. Uniqueness may arise only if additional side information restricts 

Sj to a singleton (e.g., sparsity, low-rank priors, quantization). 

Protocol constraints. To preserve confidentiality in practice, the following requirements are critical: 1. Fixed sketch per matrix. For each W , the cloud publishes a single Rpub = Mpub W

with fixed Mpub . Re-issuing with linearly independent Mpub is disallowed, since stack-ing multiple sketches could collapse the null space. 2. CVM attestation and isolation. Although the CVM performs unmasking internally, the client cannot read intermediate tensors 

(E, O = EW ); otherwise, by collecting enough input–output pairs, the client could solve EW = O and reconstruct W . Talaria relies on cloud-based attestation and runtime isolation to prevent such leakage. 3. Independent masking. Distinct, untied weight matrices (e.g., WQ, W K , W V , W O)must use independent masks Mpub to avoid cross-module linkability. 4. Domain specification. The analysis assumes real-valued arithmetic. If weights are repre-sented in finite rings (e.g., Z2k ), rank and null-space properties must be verified in that alge-braic domain. 15 Conclusion. The dimensionality constraint m < d guarantees that Mpub W alone does not uniquely determine W , ensuring non-identifiability under standard assumptions. Model confidentiality thus holds provided that (i) no additional independent sketches are revealed, (ii) clients cannot access intermediate tensors, and (iii) no strong structural priors on W are externally available.

C.1.3 Attacks 

We validate our defense against three SOTA recon-struction attacks. 

Classification-based attack. TokenInfer (Luo et al., 2025) reconstructs private tokens by inter-cepting the intermediate embeddings exchanged between devices in a distributed LLM framework. The core of the attack relies on the discovery that these intermediate embeddings form distinct clus-ters corresponding to their input tokens. The ad-versary trains a classification model to map the intercepted embeddings back to their original to-kens. The attack model is a multi-layer perceptron consisting of six layers. Layer normalization and ReLU activation functions are used between con-secutive layers. The training epochs for the attack model are 10. 

Optimization-based attack. TokenInv (Qu et al., 2025) is divided into two stages: First, a con-strained optimization phase is used to recover a continuous embedding that is a close approxima-tion of the original prompt. Second, an adaptive discretization phase accurately converts this em-bedding back into discrete text tokens by using the activation data and semantic speculation from an-other LLM to improve accuracy and readability. The attack model is set as optimization learning rate to 0.1, constraint coefficient to 0.1, optimiza-tion iteration number to 1500, embedding-based candidate size to 10, and semantic-based candidate size to 10. 

Generation-based attack. SentenceInv (Gu et al., 2023) trains a decoder model using a publicly avail-able dataset from the same domain as a private tar-get dataset. The decoder learns to reconstruct the original text from its sentence embeddings, which are generated by a pre-trained language model like BERT (Devlin et al., 2019). The adversary then uses this trained decoder on the private embeddings to reconstruct the original, sensitive sentences, re-vealing high-level semantic information. The at-tack model is an adversarial decoder, which inherits from the transformer architecture with 6 decod-ing layers and 8 heads. The decoder is trained for 100 epochs with cross-entropy loss, AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov and Hutter, 2019), and a learning rate of 1e-4.
