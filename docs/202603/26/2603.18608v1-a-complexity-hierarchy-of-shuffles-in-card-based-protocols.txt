# A Complexity Hierarchy of Shuffles in Card-Based Protocols

Abstract 

Card-based cryptography uses physical playing cards to construct protocols for se-cure multi-party computation. Existing card-based protocols employ various types of shuffles, some of which are easy to implement in practice while others are considerably more complex. In this paper, we classify shuffle operations into several levels according to their implementation complexity. We motivate this hierarchy from both practical and theoretical perspectives, and prove separation results between several levels by showing that certain shuffles cannot be realized using only operations from lower levels. Finally, we propose a new complexity measure for evaluating card-based protocols based on this hierarchy. 

Keywords: card-based cryptography, secure multi-party computation, shuffles, complexity, permutation sets 

# 1 Introduction 

Card-based cryptography is a branch of cryptography that uses physical playing cards to construct cryptographic protocols. Instead of relying on computers or electronic devices, the computation is carried out through simple physical operations on cards, allowing partici-pants to perform cryptographic tasks while keeping their private information hidden. Appli-cations of card-based cryptography include secure multi-party computation, zero-knowledge proofs, and other cryptographic primitives. The study of card-based cryptography dates back to 1990, when den Boer [3] introduced the “five-card trick”, a protocol that allows two parties to compute the logical AND of their input bits using five cards. A key component of card-based protocols is the use of shuffles . Shuffles are randomized operations applied to a deck of cards in order to hide information about the positions of the cards. They play a crucial role in ensuring the security of card-based protocols, as they prevent players from learning sensitive information during the execution of the protocol. Over the years, a variety of shuffles have been proposed and used in the literature. However, not all shuffle operations are equally practical. Some shuffles are easy to perform in practice and can be implemented naturally by human players. Others, although

1.1 Related Work 

Card-based cryptography was first introduced by den Boer [3] in 1990 through the well-known five-card trick , which uses the rc operation. In 2009, Gradwohl et al. [4] proposed a card-based zero-knowledge proof protocol for Sudoku that employs the ss . In the same year, Mizuki and Sone [12] introduced the random bisection cut , which can be viewed as a special case of both rpc and pss with two piles. Subsequent works introduced additional shuffle techniques and theoretical results. In 2015, Koch et al. [7] introduced the notions of non-closed and non-uniform shuffles, which are generally difficult to implement in practice. In the same year, Ishikawa et al. [5] introduced the pss . Shinagawa et al. [21] introduced the rpc in 2017, in the context of rotating polygon cards. Nishimura et al. [16] proposed special tools for performing shuffles with piles of unequal sizes, which can realize certain non-closed shuffles. In subsequent works, Nishimura et al. [14, 15] further showed that the same tools can be used to realize certain non-uniform shuffles. In 2020, Koch and Walzer [6] proved that every uniform closed shuffle can be realized using only rc operations, although additional cards of the same size but with a different back color are required. More recently, Miyamoto and Shinagawa [9] showed that certain shuffles corresponding to automorphism groups of graphs can be realized using only pss operations, again requiring additional cards. Saito et al. [18] proved that any shuffle with rational probabilities can be realized using only rc operations with additional cards; however, the number of required cards can grow extremely large, especially when the outcome set contains more than two permutations. On the experimental side, Miyahara et al. [8] experimentally measured the time required to perform some fundamental operations in practice and compared the running time of existing card-based protocols. 

1.2 Our Contribution 

In this paper, we introduce a hierarchy of shuffle operations used in card-based protocols based on their implementation complexity. We first analyze shuffle operations from both practical and theoretical viewpoints, identifying the fundamental operations that can be reliably performed in real-world settings. Based on these observations, we classify shuffles into several levels according to the operations required to realize them. We prove separation results between these levels by showing that certain sets of permutations cannot be realized using only operations from lower levels. In particular, we establish that rc is strictly more complex than ss , and that 

rpc is strictly more complex than rc . We also show that some shuffles cannot be realized using the four fundamental operations. 2Finally, we perform an exhaustive search for small deck sizes to enumerate the per-mutation sets realizable at each level, revealing strong structural restrictions on achievable shuffles. Based on this hierarchy, we propose a new complexity measure for evaluating card-based protocols. 

# 2 Preliminaries 

In the formal computational model of card-based protocols proposed by Mizuki and Shizuya [11], a shuffle of a deck is mathematically defined by a pair (Π , F ), where Π is a set of permu-tations and F is a probability distribution over Π. A deterministic permutation can be viewed as a special case of a shuffle in which |Π| = 1, i.e. Π = {π} for some permutation π,and F assigns probability 1 to π.A shuffle is called uniform if F is the uniform distribution over Π, and is called closed 

if Π forms a subgroup of the symmetric group. In this paper, we mainly consider uniform shuffles. Unless stated otherwise, a shuffle denoted only by a set Π refers to the shuffle (Π , F ), where F is the uniform distribution over Π. Another property sometimes considered in the literature is the cyclic property [1]. However, this property is irrelevant to the notion of shuffle complexity studied in this paper. For example, the cyclic shuffle {id , (1 2)(3 4) } is more complex than the non-cyclic shuffle 

{id , (1 2) , (3 4) , (1 2)(3 4) } according to the hierarchy introduced later in this paper. Next, we will formally define the four fundamental shuffle operations. 

2.1 Scramble Shuffle 

The scramble shuffle ( ss ) rearranges a deck of n cards into a uniformly random permutation, denoted by Sn. In practice, this corresponds to scrambling the cards randomly on the table. While this is a natural operation commonly performed, the first card-based protocol to formally employ this operation is the zero-knowledge proof protocol for the Sudoku puzzle of Gradwohl et al. [4]. 

2.2 Random Cut 

The random cut ( rc ) rearranges a deck of n cards into a uniformly random cyclic shift, denoted by {id , σ, σ 2, . . . , σ n−1}, where σ = (1 2 . . . n ). In practice, this operation can be implemented by repeatedly performing a cut of the deck with randomly chosen cut positions [23]. This operation appears in the very first card-based protocol, the five-card trick of den Boer [3]. 

2.3 Pile-Scramble Shuffle 

The pile-scramble shuffle ( pss ) divides a deck of kn cards into k piles of n consecutive cards, and then rearranges the k piles according to a uniformly random permutation. Formally, we index the cards from 1 to kn according to their positions in the deck. The pss is denoted by 

{πτ | τ ∈ Sk},

where πτ (( i − 1) n + j) = ( τ (i) − 1) n + j for 1 ≤ i ≤ k and 1 ≤ j ≤ n.3In practice, this operation can be implemented by placing the n cards of each pile into an envelope and applying the ss to the pile of k envelopes. Alternatively, one may use a rubber band or paper clip to tie the cards in each pile together before scrambling them. This operation was first introduced by Ishikawa et al. [5]. 

2.4 Random Pile Cut 

The random pile cut ( rpc ), also known as a pile-shifting shuffle [2] or a random k-section cut [17], divides a deck of kn cards into k piles of n consecutive cards, and then rearranges the k piles according to a uniformly random cyclic shift. Formally, it is denoted by 

{id , σ n, σ 2n, . . . , σ (k−1) n},

where σ = (1 2 . . . kn ). Like the pss , the rpc can be implemented in practice by placing the n cards of each pile into an envelope and applying the rc to the pile of k envelopes. Alternatively, one may use a rubber band or paper clip to tie the cards in each pile together before performing the cut. This operation was first introduced by Shinagawa et al. [21]. In 2020, Koch and Walzer [6] showed that the rpc can be implemented without en-velopes, rubber bands, or paper clips; however, their method requires k additional cards of the same size but with a different back color. 

# 3 Practical Viewpoint 

When designing card-based protocols, it is important to consider whether the shuffle opera-tions used in the protocol can be performed reliably in the real world. In particular, we aim to identify fundamental operations that are both easy to execute and easy for all players to verify. From a practical perspective, a fundamental operation should satisfy two main properties. 

1. The resulting permutation is unknown to everyone. After an operation is performed, no player should know the exact permutation applied to the deck, including the person performing the operation. This can be achieved through simple physical actions on the deck. One common method is to scramble the cards on the table. In this process, the cards are mixed randomly by repeatedly moving them around on the table. Although the result-ing distribution over permutations is not exactly uniform, the process can be modeled as a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is uniform over all permutations. If the scram-bling process is performed for a sufficiently long time, the resulting distribution becomes close to uniform. This operation corresponds to the ss .Another natural action is to grab a portion of the deck and move it to another position. If the insertion position is chosen arbitrarily, repeated applications of this operation also converge to a distribution close to that produced by the ss . However, if the operation is restricted so that cards are moved only in cyclic order (the bottom portion of the deck is moved to the top, or vice versa), the resulting permutation becomes a cyclic shift of the deck. Although a single cut does not necessarily produce a uniform distribution, if the deck is cut several times with independently chosen cut positions, the resulting process can be 4viewed as a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is uniform over all cyclic shifts. After sufficiently many cuts, the distribution becomes close to uniform. This operation corresponds to the rc .Thus, among simple physical actions that naturally hide the resulting permutation from all players, the two fundamental operations are the ss and the rc .

2. All players can verify that no cheating occurs. In addition to hiding the per-mutation, it must also be possible for all players to verify that the shuffler performs only permutations from the allowed set Π. If the shuffler were able to secretly apply permutations outside Π, the security of the protocol could be compromised. From this perspective, the ss is particularly easy to verify. Since the allowed set Π consists of all permutations, every possible outcome of the scrambling process is valid. Therefore, players only need to observe that the cards are being mixed; there is no need to verify the exact permutation applied. In contrast, the rc is slightly more difficult to verify. In this case, the allowed set Π consists only of cyclic shifts. Players must therefore ensure that the shuffler performs only a cyclic cut and does not secretly apply any other permutation. This requires closer observation during the operation. 

Conclusion. Combining these two considerations, we conclude that the ss is the easiest fundamental operation to perform and verify in practice, while the rc is the second easiest. In contrast, the pss and the rpc typically require extra tools such as envelopes, rubber bands, paper clips, or cards of the same size but with a different back color, and therefore cannot be performed as easily using only the cards from a given deck.

4.1 Levels of Shuffles 

We now formally classify shuffles (with uniform distributions over sets of permutations) into levels according to the operations required to realize them. 

• Level 0 shuffles are those obtainable using only deterministic permutations. Therefore, they consist only of single-element sets. 

• Level 1 shuffles are those obtainable using ss operations and deterministic permuta-tions. 

• Level 2 shuffles are those obtainable using rc operations and deterministic permuta-tions. For example, when n = 3, the shuffle {id , (1 2 3) , (1 2 3) 2} belongs to Level 2 since it can be realized using rc operations but cannot be realized using only ss operations. Next, we will prove that the rpc is strictly more complex than the rc . We do so by showing that there exists a set of permutations obtainable using the rpc that cannot be realized using only rc operations (and deterministic permutations), as stated in Theorem 2.

4.2 Shuffles Beyond the Four Fundamental Operations 

Some shuffles cannot be realized using the four fundamental operations. One example is the shuffle {id , (1 2 3) }.At first glance, one might attempt to implement this shuffle using an operation similar to the rpc (or pss ), by dividing the deck into two piles of unequal size: one pile containing two cards and the other containing one card, and then randomly swapping the two piles. However, such an operation is not secure in practice. If rubber bands or paper clips are used to hold the piles together, players can distinguish the piles during the shuffle because they contain different numbers of cards. If envelopes are used instead, the players can still determine which pile is which after the cards are taken out of the envelopes. Therefore, this approach does not achieve a secure shuffle. In fact, it is theoretically impossible to realize the shuffle {id , (1 2 3) } using only rpc 

operations and deterministic permutations, as shown in Theorem 3.

# 5 Complexity Measurement of Protocol 

Using the hierarchy of shuffle operations introduced in this paper, we propose a way to measure the shuffle complexity of card-based protocols. The goal is to evaluate protocols based on the types of shuffle operations they require. Given a protocol, we represent its shuffle complexity by a tuple (a1, a 2, a 3, a 4, a 5),

where each component counts the number of fundamental operations of a particular type used in the protocol. Specifically: 

• a1 is the number of ss operations, 

• a2 is the number of rc operations, 

• a3 is the number of pss or rpc operations, 

• a4 is the number of unequal shuffles introduced by Nishimura et al. [16], 

• a5 counts any other shuffle operations not covered by previous categories. This representation is motivated by the observation that the practical difficulty of im-plementing shuffle operations can vary significantly depending on the type of shuffle and the method used to perform it. For example, the time required to perform a rpc operation may differ greatly depending on whether envelopes, rubber bands, or additional cards are used. Therefore, representing complexity by counting the types of shuffle operations provides a more flexible way to compare protocols than assigning fixed time costs. Previous experimental work by Miyahara et al. [8] attempted to measure the running time of card-based protocols by experimentally recording the exact time required to perform 9them in practice. Their approach focused on basic actions such as add (adding cards), turn 

(turning cards), perm (deterministic permutation), and shuffle (any shuffle). However, they did not explicitly distinguish between different types of shuffle operations, although they briefly noted that under some implementations, performing the random bisection cut can take more than four times as long as performing the rc .Our approach differs in that we explicitly distinguish different classes of shuffle opera-tions according to the hierarchy introduced in this paper. Instead of measuring the exact execution time in seconds, we represent the shuffle complexity of a protocol as the tuple (a1, a 2, a 3, a 4, a 5), which records the number of operations of each type. This representa-tion allows protocols to be compared in terms of the structural complexity of the shuffle operations they require.

# 6 Future Work 

In this paper, we classified shuffles into several levels according to their implementation complexity. One possible future work is to find explicit criteria for determining whether a given set of permutations can be realized by shuffles in a particular level.
